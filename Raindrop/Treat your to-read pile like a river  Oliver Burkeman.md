# Treat your to-read pile like a river | Oliver Burkeman

![](https://static.wixstatic.com/media/053ea9_423193248b6742e282afcb4ca15c2aab%7Emv2.jpg/v1/fit/w_2500,h_1330,al_c/053ea9_423193248b6742e282afcb4ca15c2aab%7Emv2.jpg)





To return to information overload: this means treating your "to read" pile like a river (a stream that flows past you, and from which you pluck a few choice items, here and there) instead of a bucket (which demands that you empty it).
After all, you presumably don't feel overwhelmed by all the unread books in the British Library – and not because there aren't an overwhelming number of them, but because it never occurred to you that it might be your job to get through them all.
Coming at life this way definitely entails tough choices. But it's liberating, too, as you slowly begin to grasp that you never had any other option. There's no point beating yourself up for failing to clear a backlog (of unread books, undone tasks, unrealized dreams) that it was always inherently unfeasible to clear in the first place. I like to think of it as the productivity technique to beat all productivity techniques: finally internalizing the implications of the fact that what's genuinely impossible – the clue is in the name! – cannot actually be done.
